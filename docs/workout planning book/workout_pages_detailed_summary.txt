Detailed summary of the uploaded workout pages

Source: pages from a “Weight Training for Women” book, covering weekly training schedules and sample total-body, upper-body, and lower-body workouts.

1. How the program is organized

The book gives workout templates for people who can train 3, 4, or 5 days per week. The workouts are split into:

- Total-body workouts
- Upper-body workouts
- Lower-body workouts
- Cardio days
- Rest days

Each strength workout follows the same general format:

1) Warm-up
- Usually 3 to 5 movements.
- Usually 30 to 60 seconds per movement.
- No rest between warm-up moves.
- Equipment: usually none.

2) Main workout
- Usually 3 to 5 sets.
- Usually 5 to 8 reps.
- Rest between sets is usually 1 to 2 minutes.
- Tempo is slow, moderate, or fast depending on the workout.
- Equipment may be none, medium/heavy dumbbells, barbell, or kettlebell.

3) Cooldown
- Usually 3 to 5 stretches.
- Each stretch is usually 30 to 60 seconds.
- No rest between cooldown moves.
- Equipment: none.

The book also notes that if the goal is to build muscle, you can do only 1 set at first, then move straight to the next exercise. Repeat this pattern for 3 to 5 total sets. The book says supersets can increase the fat-burning effect of strength training.

6. What matters most for your goal: reducing diastolic blood pressure

Your goal is not just weight loss. Your main goal is to lower diastolic blood pressure safely while building lean muscle.

For that goal, the best approach from these pages is:

- Use the 3-day strength plan, not the 5-day plan.
- Add cardio or brisk walking on 2 days.
- Keep strength training moderate.
- Avoid breath-holding.
- Avoid very heavy lifting at the start.
- Add wall sits because they are especially relevant for blood pressure, even though wall sits are not the main focus of these pages.

Suggested weekly plan for you:

Monday: lower body, easier version
- Warm-up: 5 minutes easy walk
- Step-ups: 2 to 3 sets of 8 each leg
- Goblet squat or bodyweight squat: 2 to 3 sets of 8 to 10
- Glute bridge: 2 to 3 sets of 10 to 12
- Wall sit: 2 to 3 rounds of 20 to 40 seconds
- Cooldown: calf stretch, quad stretch, hamstring stretch

Tuesday: cardio
- Brisk walk 30 to 45 minutes
- Intensity: you can talk, but not sing

Wednesday: upper body, easier version
- Incline push-ups: 2 to 3 sets of 6 to 10
- Dumbbell row or resistance-band row: 2 to 3 sets of 8 to 10
- Light shoulder press or chest press: 2 sets of 8
- Plank: 2 to 3 rounds of 15 to 30 seconds
- Cooldown: chest stretch, shoulder stretch, child’s pose

Thursday: rest or easy walk
- 20 to 30 minutes easy walking if you want

Friday: total body, easier version
- Warm-up: 5 minutes easy walk
- Squat: 2 to 3 sets of 8 to 10
- Step-up: 2 sets of 8 each leg
- Incline push-up: 2 sets of 6 to 10
- Row: 2 sets of 8 to 10
- Wall sit: 2 rounds of 20 to 40 seconds
- Cooldown stretches

Saturday: cardio
- Brisk walk 30 to 60 minutes
- Optional: walk-jog intervals only if your blood pressure is stable and you feel good

Sunday: rest

7. Exercises from the book that are useful for you

Most useful:
- Step-up
- Squat or goblet squat
- Glute bridge
- Wall sit, added for blood pressure
- Incline push-up, as an easier substitute for chest press
- Bent-over row or resistance-band row
- Plank

Useful but be careful:
- Deadlift
- Good morning
- Romanian deadlift
- Bulgarian split squat
- Curtsy lunge
- Overhead press
- Heavy barbell chest press

These are not bad exercises, but they require more form control. They can be harder for beginners and may be too much if your blood pressure is not stable.

8. Simple rules when using this program

- Start with 2 to 3 sets, not 5 sets.
- Start with bodyweight or light dumbbells.
- Rest 1 to 2 minutes between sets.
- Breathe normally. Do not hold your breath.
- Stop if you feel dizzy, chest pain, unusual shortness of breath, or strong headache.
- Do not chase soreness. The goal is consistency.
- For blood pressure, moderate and steady training is better than hard and intense training.
- Track morning blood pressure 3 to 4 days per week.
- Track waist measurement every 2 weeks.
- Increase slowly.

9. Bottom-line summary

The book provides a solid strength-training structure. It is organized around warm-up, strength sets, and cooldown stretches. For your goal, the best choice is the 3-day strength plan plus 2 cardio days. The 4-day and 5-day plans may be too much at the beginning.

The best starting plan is:

- 3 strength days per week
- 2 brisk walking days per week
- 2 rest or easy recovery days per week
- Add wall sits 3 days per week
- Keep all lifting light to moderate
- Focus on breathing, consistency, and blood pressure trend

This is safer and more useful for reducing diastolic blood pressure than jumping into a half-marathon or heavy lifting plan.
